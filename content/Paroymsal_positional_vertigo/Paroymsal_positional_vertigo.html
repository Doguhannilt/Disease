Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo Information

What is Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo?

Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (PPV) is a type of vertigo, a false sensation of spinning or rotational movement, that occurs with certain head positions or movements. It is a common form of vertigo and usually lasts for a short duration. PPV is typically caused by the displacement of tiny calcium crystals in the inner ear (vestibular system), leading to temporary balance disturbances.

Symptoms of Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo

Common symptoms of paroxysmal positional vertigo may include:

  • Episodes of dizziness or spinning sensation
  • Vertigo triggered by changes in head position (e.g., lying down, rolling over in bed)
  • Feeling unsteady or off-balance
  • Nausea
  • Some individuals may experience nystagmus (involuntary eye movements)

Causes and Risk Factors

The exact cause of PPV is not always clear, but it is often related to the displacement of calcium crystals (otoconia) in the inner ear. Risk factors for developing paroxysmal positional vertigo may include:

  • Head injury
  • Inner ear infections
  • Age-related degeneration of the inner ear
  • Prolonged bed rest or immobility

Treatment and Management

Paroxysmal positional vertigo is usually treatable and may resolve on its own over time. Treatment options may include:

  • Canalith repositioning procedures (e.g., Epley maneuver)
  • Vestibular rehabilitation exercises
  • Medications to control symptoms (e.g., anti-nausea medications)
